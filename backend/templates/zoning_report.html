Glossary

ADU (Accessory Dwelling Unit)
A secondary residential unit on a single-family lot, such as a coach house or basement apartment. Chicago's ADU ordinance allows these in certain zones to increase housing supply.
Arm's-Length Transaction
A sale between unrelated parties acting in their own self-interest, at fair market value. This report excludes non-arm's-length sales (family transfers, foreclosures, sub-$10K transactions) from comparable analysis.
ARO (Affordable Requirements Ordinance)
Chicago ordinance requiring residential developments of 10+ units receiving city financial assistance or zoning changes to set aside a percentage of units as affordable, or pay a fee into the Affordable Housing Fund.
EZ (Enterprise Zone)
State-designated areas offering tax incentives to encourage business investment and job creation, including sales tax exemptions on building materials, investment tax credits, and utility tax exemptions.
FAR (Floor Area Ratio)
The ratio of a building's total floor area to the lot size. A FAR of 2.0 on a 5,000 sq ft lot allows up to 10,000 sq ft of floor area. FAR is the primary density control in Chicago's zoning code (Title 17).
Lot Coverage
The percentage of a lot that may be covered by buildings and structures. Limits the building footprint to preserve open space, light, and air access.
OZ (Opportunity Zone)
Federally designated census tracts where investors can receive capital gains tax benefits for long-term investments. Created by the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act to stimulate economic development in distressed communities.
PD (Planned Development)
A site-specific zoning designation requiring City Council approval. PDs allow flexibility in development standards but add a discretionary review process. Any change to an existing PD requires a new approval.
PIN (Property Index Number)
A 14-digit identifier assigned by the Cook County Assessor to every parcel of real property. Used for tax assessment, title records, and government data systems.
Setback
The minimum required distance between a building and a property line (front, side, or rear). Setback requirements vary by zoning district and ensure adequate spacing between structures.
SFHA (Special Flood Hazard Area)
FEMA-designated zones with a 1% or greater annual chance of flooding (the "100-year floodplain"). Properties in SFHAs require flood insurance if federally financed and face additional construction requirements.
SSA (Special Service Area)
A defined geographic district where businesses pay an additional property tax to fund enhanced services such as streetscaping, marketing, snow removal, and security beyond standard city services.
TIF (Tax Increment Financing)
A public financing tool that captures the increase in property tax revenue within a designated district over a base year to fund improvements within that district. Chicago TIF districts have a 23-year lifespan.
TOD (Transit-Oriented Development)
Development within a specified distance of a transit station (typically 1/4 mile for bus, 1/2 mile for rail). Chicago's TOD ordinance allows reduced parking requirements and density bonuses near transit.

Methodology

This report aggregates publicly available government data from multiple sources. Zoning standards are extracted from the indexed Chicago Municipal Code via AI (Claude Haiku) and should be verified against the current ordinance. Property assessments are triennial and may not reflect current market values. Demographics are estimated from ACS 5-year survey data (2019–2023). Comparable sales are filtered for arm's-length transactions only (excluding multi-sales, sub-$10K, and non-standard deed types). Development potential is calculated as FAR × lot area and does not account for planned development restrictions, variance requirements, or site-specific conditions.
